    Each plant in the landscape has its own growth habits and different requirements for pruning. Some shrubs have a dwarf growth habit and may never need pruning, while vigorous large-growing shrubs may require frequent pruning. Improper pruning or pruning at the wrong time of the year can cause plants to deform, reduce flowering or plants are more susceptible to damage from insects, diseases or winter cold.

    Basic

     

    First of all, in order to successfully build our plants, we need a pair of clean and sharp garden shears and some basic knowledge of plants.
    We need to build off the extra branches and leaves to help the trunk of the plant to absorb enough nutrients to grow. Doing this from time to time is very healthy for the plant. In peak seasons for plant growth, such as spring and summer, pruning plants can get the most benefit. This allows the plant to grow more fully and eliminate any yellowing or dead parts. Whether you want to maintain a certain size, encourage branching, or gain a certain appearance, pruning is one of the best ways to control the growth of plants.
    But be careful when pruning. According to the habits of different plants, for example, some plants will never produce branches, and the wrong way of constructing may cause the plants to die.

     

    Seasonal vs. Daily Pruning

     

    It is best to prune plants in spring or summer. Due to the right temperature and humidity, this period is the fastest time for most plants to grow. During this time, you can usually trim off a lot of leaves, branches, or anything that will reduce the number of plants. But please note that when pruning, if you plan to cut off the larger stems, please move as close to the main stem as possible, and it is best not to remove more than 1/4 of the total leaves of the plant.
    In the fall and winter, do not perform any major pruning because your plants will not grow at such a fast rate, and it may take longer for them to achieve new growth or recover from excessively pruned plants.
    However, some tasks can be easily completed at any time of the year. Peel off the yellowish or brown leaves and trim off some stems or vines.

     

    Shearing vs. Thinning

     

    When shrubs are routinely pruned, many dense, dense new growths are produced near the outside of the crown. As a result, less light reaches the plant's interior, the leaves in the crown become sparse, and the plant appears cauline or hollow.
    Refinement is usually preferable to pruning. Sparseness promotes new growth inside shrubs, reduces the size and provides fuller, more attractive plants.

     

    Making the Cut

     

    When you cut the branch back into the trunk, side branch, or side bud, the higher concentration of hormones in these areas causes the wound to heal quickly. As you move away from the root, the distance from the source of the hormone increases, and if it heals completely, the wound will heal more slowly. Insects and diseases may enter the incised part of the root and kill it.
    Whether you are pruning small branches or large branches, you can avoid leaving residual roots by always cutting back to the bud, side branch, or trunk.

     

    Shaping Tree-Form Shrubs

     

    Common landscape shrubs can be shaped into tree forms (shrubs shaped like a tree with one or more main trunks). Plants such a crape myrtle, yaupon holly, wax myrtle and wax-leaf ligustrum are commonly pruned to tree forms. The best time to begin a tree form is early March before spring growth begins.
    You can prune from a single trunk into a multi-trunk treelike structure by pruning to the ground, selecting the three to five most vigorous new shoots during the growing season as the main trunk, and then removing all the other trunks.
